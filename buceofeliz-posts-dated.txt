Dove the Lady Luck in Pompano Beach Florida.

The Lady Luck, a 324-foot-long decommissioned tanker, is a prominent artificial reef located off the coast of Pompano Beach, Florida. It is one of the most famous dive sites in the area, known for its unique and carefully designed underwater experience that captivates divers from around the world.

Originally named Newtown Creek, the Lady Luck began her life as a sludge tanker, transporting liquid waste for New York City. Built in 1967, she played a vital role in the city\u2019s waste management system for several decades before retiring from service. In 2016, the tanker was repurposed as an artificial reef as part of the Shipwreck Park project, a collaborative effort to boost marine life and promote tourism in South Florida.

The sinking of the Lady Luck was a carefully orchestrated event, attended by divers, locals, and marine enthusiasts. Since her transformation into an underwater attraction, the shipwreck has become a hotspot for marine life and recreational divers, serving as a vivid reminder of how human intervention can contribute to marine conservation while also providing an unforgettable dive experience.

Lady Luck is an advanced dive site, given its depth and the complexity of the wreck. The shipwreck sits upright on the sandy seafloor at a depth of around 120 feet (36 meters), with the main deck located at about 85 feet (26 meters). This makes it ideal for divers with wreck diving or deep diving certifications, though some areas of the wreck can be safely explored by advanced open water divers who are comfortable at greater depths.

The Lady Luck wreck was not just sunk as an ordinary vessel; it was creatively transformed into an underwater casino, complete with poker tables, slot machines, and card sharks. Artists and designers from the South Florida community contributed to these features, adding an element of humor and intrigue to the dive. The ship's wheelhouse is also accessible, and divers can take photos while pretending to captain the vessel.

Inside the ship, there are numerous swim-throughs and compartments to explore, though divers are advised to exercise caution and avoid areas with limited visibility. Penetration dives should only be attempted by those trained in wreck diving and equipped with proper gear, including reels and redundant air supplies.

One of the major goals of the Lady Luck wreck was to create an environment conducive to marine life. Over the years, it has become a thriving habitat for various species. Divers can expect to see schools of fish, barracudas, goliath groupers, and tropical reef fish, such as angelfish and parrotfish, swimming around the ship. The structure also attracts a variety of invertebrates like sea urchins, sponges, and corals, making it a vibrant underwater ecosystem.

Seasonal visitors such as sea turtles and rays may also be spotted, and the wreck is home to some larger pelagic fish that use the artificial reef as a hunting ground. Photographers and marine life enthusiasts find plenty of opportunities to capture breathtaking scenes and observe the dynamic marine community that the Lady Luck supports.

* Depth: The Lady Luck sits at a maximum depth of 120 feet (36 meters), with the deck at around 85 feet (26 meters). This is considered a deep dive and requires adequate training, experience, and equipment.

* Certification: Advanced Open Water certification is the minimum requirement, and additional wreck or deep diving certifications are highly recommended.

* Conditions: Currents can be strong, and visibility can vary from 30 to 80 feet (9 to 24 meters) depending on weather and sea conditions. The best time to dive is typically in the morning when the waters are calmer.
   
* Preparation: Due to the depth, divers should monitor their air consumption carefully and make safety stops during ascent to minimize the risk of decompression sickness. A dive computer and redundant air supply are essential for safety.

Diving the Lady Luck is a unique experience, combining the thrill of a deep shipwreck dive with the whimsy of an underwater casino. It stands as a testament to marine conservation efforts and the adventurous spirit of divers worldwide. For those seeking an unforgettable adventure beneath the waves, the Lady Luck offers a dive full of history, creativity, and marine wonders, making it a must-visit site for seasoned wreck divers.

Whether you are there to explore the ship\u2019s many attractions or marvel at the marine life that now calls it home, the Lady Luck promises an adventure that is both exhilarating and rewarding.

Staying Streamlined: Why a Backup Mask, Cutting Tool, and Spool/DSMB in a Butt Pouch Make Sense

When it comes to diving, especially in technical environments, staying streamlined is key. Whether you're navigating tight spaces or just trying to reduce drag, every piece of gear needs to have its place. One of the most practical solutions for staying streamlined while keeping essential gear accessible is using a butt pouch. In my case, I picked up an XDeep butt pouch during my cave diving course, and it's been a game-changer. Let me tell you why you should consider adding one to your kit.

A butt pouch allows you to carry critical backup gear\u2014like a mask, cutting tool, and spool/DSMB\u2014without cluttering the front of your setup. It sits snugly against your backplate, so you don\u2019t feel it during the dive, and it\u2019s easy to access when needed. I\u2019ve added some bungee rings to the backplate to clip onto while diving backmount BPW (Backplate and Wing), and the whole setup stays clean and streamlined.

One of the benefits of a butt pouch is its simplicity. You don\u2019t have to mess around with bulky side pockets or worry about stuff flapping around during your dive. Everything stays neatly packed away until you need it. This is especially useful for technical diving, where minimal drag and easy access to tools can be the difference between a smooth dive and a frustrating one.

However, if you\u2019re like me and don\u2019t want to spend a fortune on a brand-name pouch, you can go the DIY route. Food for thought.  

One of the more awkward yet crucial safety skills students stumble with is the low pressure inflator hose disconnect drill.

The last time I taught for another dive shop the rental gear was so corroded the student couldn't possibly learn the skill.  I myself had a hard time disconnecting the inflator hose.  

This was frustrating for them, and for me.  It's such an important potentially life saving skill. A failed runaway inflater is one of the most common problems that can cause an uncontrolled rapid ascent.  

So something I always do before I teach is to make sure the inflator hose my student has is easy and smooth to operate correctly.

Regular cleaning with vinegar or a dip in the ultrasonic plus a small dab of silicone applied with the tip of a probe keeps things moving as they should.   

You may wonder why I'm more expensive to study with than the cattle boat operators.  This is one of the reasons.   I make the skills easier to learn because I put more time into the details.  Here's all it takes not to cut corners.  But that costs more because it takes more time in and out of the water and behind the scenes.

Tiny Arrow Crab at night.   Shot with an Olympus TG-6 and a dive light.

Sea Anenome Diptic.  Up close and personal.  Their venom is super complex with at least 17 different molecules.  While most human envenomations are harmless they can cause a pruritic rash.  But to their prey the venom is deadly.  There are two different types of cells that produce the toxins.  

Nematocytes that shoot little barbs and trigger a venom release upon contact are the same type of cell found in jellyfish and fire corals.  And then there are these other weird cells, ectodermal gland cells that release a toxin into the water that also assists with disabling and catching prey.  

The reason why sea anemone toxins are located in two different types of cells remains unknown. However, secretion by gland cells may allow for delivery of larger amounts of the toxin, and present an opportunity to extend the reach of venom use by the anemone beyond direct contact.

So interesting

All clownfish are born male.

There\u2019s a good reason why both main characters in Finding Nemo are male, at least initially. All clownfish are born that way, and it\u2019s only when a group\u2019s dominant female dies or disappears that a male will develop into a female and become the new matriarch. All clownfish have the ability to turn female, and the change is permanent once it occurs. The transformation begins almost immediately after the dominant female leaves, and starts in the brain before manifesting itself in the sex organs. Had the beloved Pixar film been devoted to scientific accuracy, Nemo\u2019s father, Marlin, might not have been just his sole caregiver after tragedy befalls the boy\u2019s mother \u2014 he might literally have become his mother.

Clownfish aren\u2019t the only reef-dwellers that can change sex. The bluehead wrasse does it as well, only in reverse: When a dominant male leaves its group, the largest female transforms into a male over the course of just 21 days. Researchers have identified no fewer than 500 fish species capable of changing sex; some, like the coral-dwelling species of gobies, can even switch back and forth. The process is believed to have reproductive benefits, as it allows a single fish to reproduce as both sexes throughout its life.
